Chapter 1 of 4

Fundamentals of Pathology

Pathology is the scientific study of disease. Learn the terminology and the four core aspects that define the disease process.

🔬

Topic 1.1: Introduction & The Core of Pathology

Pathology (from Greek *pathos* = suffering, and *logos* = study) bridges clinical practice and basic science. It involves the investigation of the causes, mechanisms, and effects of disease.

The Four Core Aspects of Pathology
1Etiology (Cause): Why a disease arises. Can be genetic (e.g., mutations) or acquired (e.g., infectious, nutritional, chemical).
2Pathogenesis (Mechanism): The sequence of events from the initial stimulus to the ultimate expression of the disease. *How* the disease develops.
3Morphologic Changes: The structural alterations in cells or tissues that are characteristic of the disease or diagnostic of the etiologic process.
4Clinical Significance: The functional consequences of morphologic changes. This leads to the clinical manifestations (signs and symptoms) observed in the patient.
📖 Terminology: Cytology vs. Biopsy

Cytology: The examination of individual cells or clusters of cells (e.g., Pap smear) to detect abnormalities.
Biopsy: The removal and examination of a small piece of living tissue for diagnostic purposes.

Chapter 2 of 4

Cell Injury & Cellular Responses

How cells adapt to stress, what happens when they fail to adapt (injury), and the ultimate outcome of irreversible injury (necrosis and gangrene).

🧬

Topic 2.1: Cellular Adaptations

Cells constantly adjust their structure and function to accommodate changing demands. These reversible changes are called **cellular adaptations**.

Adaptation Type Definition Clinical Example
Hyperplasia Increase in the number of cells in an organ or tissue. Thickening of skin due to chronic friction (callus); hormonal breast growth.
Hypertrophy Increase in the size of individual cells, resulting in increased organ size. Skeletal muscle enlargement in bodybuilders; left ventricular hypertrophy in hypertension.
Atrophy Decrease in cell size and organ size due to loss of cell substance. Muscle wasting due to a broken limb in a cast (disuse atrophy).
Metaplasia Reversible replacement of one adult cell type by another adult cell type better able to withstand stress. Squamous metaplasia in the respiratory tract of chronic smokers.
☠️

Topic 2.2: Necrosis & Gangrene

If the stress is too severe, cells suffer irreversible injury leading to cell death. Necrosis is the pathological form of cell death characterized by severe membrane damage, lysosomal enzyme leakage, and local inflammation.

🔴 Exam Alert — Gangrene Types

Gangrene is not a specific pattern of cell death, but a clinical term often applied to a limb that has lost its blood supply and undergone necrosis.

• Dry Gangrene: Coagulative necrosis due to ischemia (e.g., frostbite). Tissue is dry, black, and shriveled.
• Wet Gangrene: Occurs when bacterial infection supervenes on necrotic tissue. Liquefactive necrosis makes it swollen, foul-smelling, and moist.
• Gas Gangrene: Caused by *Clostridium perfringens* bacteria emitting gas into the tissues.

Chapter 3 of 4

Inflammation & Repair

The body's protective response to eliminate the initial cause of injury and the subsequent process of tissue healing.

🔥

Topic 3.1: Acute vs. Chronic Inflammation

Inflammation is a vital response of vascularized tissues that delivers leukocytes and molecules of host defense from the circulation to the sites of infection and cell damage.

Comparison: Acute vs. Chronic
DurationAcute: Fast (minutes to days).
Chronic: Slow (days to years).
Cellular InfiltrateAcute: Mainly Neutrophils.
Chronic: Monocytes/Macrophages and Lymphocytes.
Tissue InjuryAcute: Usually mild and self-limited.
Chronic: Often severe and progressive.
Local/Systemic SignsAcute: Prominent clinical signs (Heat, Redness, Swelling, Pain, Loss of function).
Chronic: Less prominent.
🩹

Topic 3.2: Healing by First vs. Second Intention

Repair restores tissue architecture and function. It occurs via two main pathways depending on the severity of the wound.

  • Healing by First Intention (Primary Union): Occurs in clean, uninfected surgical incisions approximated by sutures. There is minimal tissue loss, rapid epithelialization, and very little scar formation.
  • Healing by Second Intention (Secondary Union): Occurs in large wounds with extensive tissue loss (e.g., ulcers, abscesses). The edges cannot be brought together. Healing involves intense inflammation, abundant granulation tissue formation, wound contraction, and substantial scarring.
💡 Factors Affecting Wound Healing

Healing can be impaired by Local factors (infection, foreign bodies, poor blood supply to the area) or Systemic factors (diabetes, poor nutrition/Vitamin C deficiency, immunosuppression, age).

Hemodynamic Disorders

Disorders involving disruptions to normal blood flow, fluid balance, and blood coagulation.

🩸

Topic 4.1: Edema, Thrombosis & Embolism

Condition Pathological Definition
Edema The accumulation of abnormal amounts of fluid in the interstitial tissue spaces or body cavities (e.g., due to increased hydrostatic pressure or decreased plasma osmotic pressure).
Thrombosis The inappropriate formation of a solid mass (thrombus) from the constituents of blood within the intact cardiovascular system.
Embolism A detached intravascular solid, liquid, or gaseous mass that is carried by the blood from its point of origin to a distant site, where it often causes tissue dysfunction or infarction.
Infarction An area of ischemic necrosis caused by occlusion of either the arterial supply or the venous drainage.
